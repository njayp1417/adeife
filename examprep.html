Key Study Notes

Nigerian Constitution (1999)

Key Features:

  • Federal system with 36 states and FCT
  • Presidential system of government
  • Separation of powers (Executive, Legislative, Judicial)
  • Fundamental human rights (Chapter IV)
  • Directive principles of state policy

Amendment Process: Requires 2/3 majority in National Assembly and approval by 2/3 of state assemblies

Arms of Government

Executive:

  • President (Head of State and Government)
  • Vice President
  • Federal Executive Council (Ministers)
  • Governors and Deputy Governors (States)

Legislature:

  • National Assembly (Senate + House of Representatives)
  • State Houses of Assembly
  • Local Government Councils

Judiciary:

  • Supreme Court (Highest court)
  • Court of Appeal
  • Federal High Court, State High Courts
  • Magistrate and Customary Courts

Electoral System

INEC (Independent National Electoral Commission):

  • Conducts federal elections
  • Registers political parties
  • Voter registration and education

Election Types:

  • Presidential (every 4 years)
  • National Assembly (Senate & House of Reps)
  • Governorship and State Assembly
  • Local Government elections

Practice Questions

WAEC/NECO Style Questions

1. The 1999 Constitution of Nigeria can be amended by

A. Simple majority in National Assembly

B. Two-thirds majority in National Assembly only

C. Two-thirds majority in National Assembly and approval by two-thirds of State Assemblies

D. Referendum by the people

Answer: C

2. Which of the following is NOT a feature of federalism?

A. Division of powers between levels of government

B. Written constitution

C. Unitary system of government

D. Independent judiciary

Answer: C

1. The principle of separation of powers was advocated by

A. John Locke

B. Montesquieu

C. Thomas Hobbes

D. Jean Jacques Rousseau

Key Study Notes

Literary Devices

Figures of Speech:

  • Metaphor: Direct comparison without "like" or "as"
  • Simile: Comparison using "like" or "as"
  • Personification: Giving human qualities to non-human things
  • Alliteration: Repetition of initial consonant sounds
  • Hyperbole: Deliberate exaggeration
  • Irony: Contrast between expectation and reality

Poetic Forms

Sonnet: 14-line poem with specific rhyme scheme

Ballad: Narrative poem, often set to music

Ode: Lyrical poem expressing deep feelings

Elegy: Mournful poem, often about death

Free Verse: Poetry without regular meter or rhyme

Dramatic Elements

Plot Structure:

  • Exposition (introduction)
  • Rising Action (conflict development)
  • Climax (turning point)
  • Falling Action (resolution begins)
  • Denouement (conclusion)

Types of Drama: Tragedy, Comedy, History, Romance
